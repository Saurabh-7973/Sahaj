# Pelvic Floor Muscles and Erection Control

Let me start with something reassuring: the muscles we're going to talk about respond to training, and you can work on them quietly, on your own, without any equipment, clinic visit, or anything anyone in the house would notice.

Your pelvic floor is a sheet of muscle slung between your pubic bone and your tailbone, like a hammock at the base of your pelvis. It helps you control urine and wind, and it also plays a part in erections and in the timing of ejaculation. Two of these muscles in particular — the ischiocavernosus and the bulbocavernosus — squeeze at the base of the penis to raise the pressure inside it, and help slow the blood that would otherwise drain away (Dorey et al., 2004). When they work well, they help an erection stay firm.

## What the evidence shows

The most useful study here is a randomised trial from the UK. Fifty-five men with erectile difficulties were split into two groups: one did pelvic floor exercises (with biofeedback, to make sure they were using the right muscles) plus general health advice; the other got the health advice alone. Over six months, a meaningful share of the exercise group regained normal erections and more improved — better than advice alone (Dorey et al., 2004).

A later review pulled together ten trials of pelvic floor training for erectile difficulties and premature ejaculation. Across the studies that looked at erections, all showed some improvement, with "cure" rates landing roughly between a third and a half of men (Myers & Smith, 2019). For ejaculation timing, an Italian study trained 40 men with lifelong premature ejaculation over 12 weeks; their average time to climax rose substantially, and most improved (Pastore et al., 2014).

Now the honest part. The same reviewers said those ten studies were of low-to-moderate quality, and that no single "best" exercise plan has been proven (Myers & Smith, 2019). European urology guidelines strike the same balance — they call pelvic floor training with biofeedback a promising option while asking for stronger trials (EAU). So this is a genuinely helpful tool for many men. It is not a guaranteed fix, and it does not replace treating an underlying health problem.

## General guidance

To find the muscles, imagine trying to stop yourself passing wind, or stopping the flow of urine midway — that lift-and-squeeze feeling is your pelvic floor. Use the urine test only *once* to locate them; don't make a habit of stopping your stream, as that can upset normal bladder emptying.

A simple approach: squeeze gently, hold for a few seconds, then fully let go for the same length of time. The release matters as much as the squeeze — a muscle that never relaxes can turn tight and sore, which doesn't help. Keep your stomach, thighs, and buttocks relaxed, and keep breathing normally. You can do a short set sitting at your desk, standing in a queue, or lying in bed. Most men who notice a difference do so after several weeks of staying consistent, so go gently and give it time.

## See a doctor if…

Pelvic floor work is for the muscular and stress-related side of things. Please get checked by a doctor if you notice:

- **No morning or night-time erections** over several weeks — usually a sign to look for a physical cause rather than a stress-related one (a useful clue, though not a perfect test)
- **A sudden change**, or erections that are steadily getting weaker
- **Signs of a metabolic problem** — a lot of thirst, passing urine often, unusual tiredness, or weight change you can't explain; erection trouble can be an early flag for diabetes or heart-and-blood-vessel disease (Cortese et al., 2023; Kouidrat et al., 2017)
- **Pain**, a curve that's new, or a lump
- **Low mood, heavy anxiety, or any thoughts of harming yourself** — this matters as much as the physical side, and help is available

None of this is a verdict on you. It's your body asking for a routine check — blood pressure, blood sugar, and an honest conversation. In India these worries often go unspoken because of stigma and shared family living, and many men put off asking for help (Kumar et al., 2018). You don't have to. Many clinics can be booked online and paid for privately by UPI.

## One gentle next step

Tonight, just find the muscle once — a single gentle squeeze and release. That's all. No counting, no targets. You've started.
