## Performance anxiety: the worry that becomes the problem

Here's the cruel trick of performance anxiety: the worry about whether your body will perform is exactly what stops it from performing. You're anxious, so you can't relax; you can't relax, so things don't work; that becomes proof to worry more next time. A loop that feeds itself.

If that's you, the first thing to know is that you are in very ordinary company. Studies have found performance anxiety affects roughly one in six men — about as common as erectile difficulty itself, and even more common among men under fifty. It's just that nobody talks about it.

### Why anxiety physically gets in the way
This isn't "in your head" in the dismissive sense — it's in your nervous system, very physically. Arousal needs your body in a calm, settled state. Anxiety flips you into the opposite — the alert, braced, "fight-or-flight" state — which actively works against blood flow and arousal. So the harder you *try* and the more you watch yourself, the more your own stress response shuts the door. Trying harder is the one thing that makes it worse.

### What actually helps — and it's well-studied
- **Reframing the whole thing.** Intimacy is not a test you pass or fail. The moment it becomes a performance to be graded, anxiety wins. Treating it as shared, unhurried, no-pressure changes the physiology.
- **Knowing what's normal.** A lot of performance anxiety runs on unrealistic expectations. Bodies vary, nights vary, and an occasional off night means nothing.
- **Cognitive behavioural techniques**, which have randomised-trial support for improving both erectile function and sexual anxiety — essentially, catching and correcting the catastrophic thoughts.
- **Sensate focus** — the classic approach of deliberately taking performance off the table and focusing on relaxed, non-demand touch and sensation. Removing the pressure, paradoxically, is what lets the body work.
- **Presence over monitoring.** Mindfulness and breathing pull your attention out of your head (where you're watching and judging) and back into the moment. This is exactly the calm-and-present skill Sahaj trains.

Most men who work on this see real improvement within a couple of months.

### An honest word for the Indian context
A lot of performance anxiety here isn't free-floating — it's manufactured by very specific pressures. The weight of an unconsummated early marriage. Family expectations around producing a child. Being expected to perform on a particular date for conception. These situations create textbook performance anxiety, and the men inside them usually carry it in total silence. If that's you: this is a known, understood, common thing, and it responds to taking the pressure *down*, not up.

### Caveats
- If performance anxiety has lasted more than a couple of months or is causing real distress, a psychosexual therapist or counsellor genuinely helps — this is one of the most treatable things there is.
- It's also worth ruling out physical contributors with a doctor, especially if the difficulty happens even when you're relaxed or alone.
