## The muscles holding it all up: your pelvic floor

Most men go their whole lives without knowing they have a pelvic floor — let alone that they can train it. It's a sling of muscle across the base of your pelvis, front to back, like a small hammock holding up your bladder and bowel. Easy to ignore, until you realise how much of men's health quietly runs through it.

Those same muscles do real work in erections and ejaculation. A couple of them wrap around the base of the penis: they help trap blood to keep an erection firm, and they fire rhythmically at the moment of ejaculation. That's the whole reason training them can matter for sexual health — not just for bladder control.

### What the research actually shows

You deserve the honest version, so here it is.

**For erections.** A randomised trial by Dorey and colleagues taught men with erectile difficulties a programme of pelvic floor exercises. After six months, roughly four in ten had regained normal erectile function, and about another third had improved. A later review that pooled the studies concluded pelvic floor training can help both erectile difficulties and premature ejaculation. It's only fair to add that these trials are mostly small, and not every study found a clear effect.

**For lasting longer.** In men with lifelong premature ejaculation, a twelve-week pelvic floor rehabilitation programme helped a large majority gain better control, with the average time to ejaculation rising substantially. Longer follow-up work has found those gains can hold for years in many men. Encouraging — and again, the highest-quality evidence is still catching up.

The honest summary: this is one of the better-supported things you can do on your own. It tends to help most with milder difficulties, it needs consistency to work, and it isn't a guaranteed fix for everyone. No supplement, no shortcut — just a muscle, trained.

### The part most people miss: both directions

Strength is only half the story. A pelvic floor that's too *tense* — gripped, never fully letting go — causes its own trouble, and is a recognised contributor to pelvic pain, urinary symptoms, and sexual difficulty. For some men the problem isn't weakness at all; it's that the muscle never relaxes. Those men actually need to learn to release and lengthen it first — sometimes with a physiotherapist — before any strengthening at all.

So good training works both ways: building strength *and* the ability to fully let go. Squeezing harder is not the goal. Control is.

### What this means for you

These are things most of us were never allowed to talk about — not at home, not with friends, often not even with a doctor. That silence helps no one. The quiet truth is that a lot of men's sexual health rests on ordinary, trainable physiology that you can work on privately, at your own pace, with no shame and without spending money on things that don't work.

A few honest caveats:
- This helps many men, but not all, and not always on its own.
- If you have pain, or things aren't improving despite consistent practice, or something simply feels wrong — that's a doctor's job, not a reason to train harder. *(See: warning signs.)*
- If you've had prostate surgery, a pelvic injury, or a neurological condition, speak to a doctor or pelvic-floor physiotherapist before you start.

Trained gently and consistently, this muscle can do a great deal for you. That's not a sales pitch — it's just what the evidence, and a good deal of clinical experience, point to.
